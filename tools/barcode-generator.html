What is a Barcode?

A barcode is a machine-readable representation of data, typically displayed as a series of parallel lines of varying widths (linear or 1D barcodes) or as patterns of squares, dots, or other geometric shapes (2D barcodes). Barcodes are used to quickly and accurately collect data, eliminating the need for manual data entry.

Common Uses of Barcodes

  • Product identification in retail stores
  • Inventory management and asset tracking
  • Shipping and logistics tracking
  • Event and ticket management
  • Patient identification in healthcare
  • Library book management
  • Manufacturing process control

Types of Barcodes We Support

UPC-A

Universal Product Code, used primarily in the United States and Canada for retail products. Contains 12 digits.

Example format: 12 numeric digits

EAN-13

European Article Number, used worldwide for retail products. Contains 13 digits.

Example format: 13 numeric digits

Code 128

High-density linear barcode that can encode all 128 ASCII characters. Used in logistics and transportation.

Example format: Any ASCII character

Code 39

Used in non-retail environments such as industrial applications and government. Can encode uppercase letters, numbers, and some special characters.

Example format: A-Z, 0-9, -, ., $, /, +, %, and space

ITF-14

Used for packaging levels in warehousing and distribution. Contains 14 digits.

Example format: 14 numeric digits

EAN-8

Compact version of EAN-13, used for small retail items where space is limited. Contains 8 digits.

Example format: 8 numeric digits

Tips for Good Barcodes

  • Ensure adequate contrast between barcode and background (black on white works best)
  • Make barcodes large enough to be scanned reliably (minimum height of 1 cm for most scanners)
  • Include appropriate quiet zones (margins) around the barcode (minimum 10x the narrowest bar)
  • Test your barcodes with the scanning devices that will be used
  • For retail products, verify that you're using the correct barcode format for your region
  • Print on non-reflective materials for better scanning
  • Avoid resizing the barcode after generation as it may affect scannability

Which barcode type should I use for my retail product?

For retail products in the USA and Canada, UPC-A is commonly used. For global retail products, EAN-13 is the standard. If you need an official retail barcode, you should obtain one through GS1, the official global barcode issuing organization.

Here's a quick guide:

  • UPC-A: Standard retail products in USA/Canada
  • EAN-13: Global retail products
  • EAN-8: Small retail items with limited space
  • Code 128: General purpose, non-retail applications
  • Code 39: Industrial and government applications
  • ITF-14: Packaging and shipping cartons

Note: For official retail barcodes to be accepted by major retailers, you must register with GS1 to obtain a company prefix and unique product numbers.

What's the difference between the download formats?

PNG: A raster image format best for web use, digital displays, and basic applications. PNG files have a fixed resolution, so they may become pixelated if enlarged too much.

SVG: A vector format that can be scaled to any size without losing quality. SVG is ideal for print materials, especially when the barcode needs to be resized. SVG files are also typically smaller in file size.

PDF: A document format that preserves formatting and is easy to print. PDFs are ideal for professional printing, documentation, and when you need to include additional information with your barcode.

For most applications, we recommend using SVG for the highest quality and flexibility.

How do I ensure my barcodes will scan properly when printed?

To ensure your barcodes scan properly when printed:

  1. Print Quality: Use a good quality printer with at least 300 DPI resolution
  2. Size: Ensure the barcode height is at least 1 cm (0.4 inches) for most scanners
  3. Contrast: Always print with high contrast, ideally black bars on a white background
  4. Quiet Zones: Leave adequate white space (quiet zones) on both sides of the barcode
  5. Materials: Print on non-glossy materials to reduce reflection
  6. Test: Scan the printed barcode with the intended scanner to verify functionality
  7. Avoid Distortion: Don't stretch or compress the barcode after generation

For best results, download the SVG format and use it in your design software without resizing the proportions.
